Chronic Pain Rehabilitation and Real World Data

Background on Chronic Pain

  • Chronic pain is defined as pain that persists or recurs for 3 months or longer

  • In 2020, approximately 619 million people globally were affected by low back pain

  • Low back pain and neck pain have constantly been one of the leading causes of disability worldwide (Measuring with years lived with disability)

  • The current gold standard for diagnosing chronic pain is patient self-report

  • Traditional medical interventions include methods such as spinal injections, surgery, and pharmacological interventions

Virtual Reality for Rehabilitation

  • Non-pharmacological interventions include physical therapy, exercise, and cognitive behavioral therapy

  • Virtual Reality (VR) has been proposed as a (supplementary) tool for rehabilitation and pain management

  • Several studies conducted under controlled trial settings have shown promising results, such as reduced pain intensity and reduced kinesiophobia (movevement-related fear of pain)

VR in a Real-World Setting

VR Rehabilitation

Study participants were given the opportunity to use a Virtual Reality (VR) headset on their own time for rehabilitation exercises.

  • Participants took part in a study with a tentative length of 12 weeks (not mandated)

  • The participants were instructed to use the VR environment freely to mimic real world usage

  • Movement data were collected using Meta Oculus Quest 2 VR equipment (e.g. \(x,y,z\) -coordinates of headset and hand controllers) at approximately 30 ms intervals

Dealing with Real-World Data

  • Monte Carlo Simulation
  • Why and How?

  • Real-world data is often messy and incomplete

  • Monte Carlo simulation can be used to assess the robustness of the results

  • Normalize picking speed to span values from 0 to 1

  • Pick 4 random participants for each round

  • Take bootstrap sample of 1001 observations from pooled data and fit regression line (Repeat 3000 rounds)

Not Really

Tip of the Day

Consult a statistician when designing a study, and talk to data professionals when planning data collection.

Conclusions

  1. Virtual Reality (VR) can effectively supplement chronic pain rehabilitation, with studies showing improvements in movement speed and reduced kinesiophobia.

  2. RWD is inherently messy and requires exploration and robust analysis methods to derive reliable insights.

  3. Study design and data collection planning are critical — unexpected issues can complicate interpretation and outcomes.
